Pre War:

Ivan Konev was conscripted into the Russian army in 1916 but demobilized one year later during the revolution. He joined The Red Army and The Bolshevik Party in 1918, serving as Military Commissar of the Nikolsk District, and later as part of the 17th Maritime Corps during The Russian Civil War. Konev completed advanced officer training in 1926 and was made a corps commander in 1937.

WWII:

Konev was in Vitebsk when The Nazis invaded. He organized a tactical retreat through Smolensk to Moscow. Konev was promoted to Colonel-General after playing a key role in The Defense of Moscow. He lead the southern forces during The Battle of Kursk in 1943, before being sent to Ukraine. Konev was made Marshal of the Soviet Union in 1944 after his army retook Belgorod, Odessa, Kharkiv, and Kiev. He and Georgy Zhukov coordinated a massive offensive in the winter of 1945 that pushed The Germans out of Poland, allowed The Red Army to Capture Konigsberg, and assisted in The Battle of Berlin. While Zhukov was given the honor of taking Berlin, Konev was sent to Czechoslovakia, where his forces fought in The The Battle of Prague.

Post War:

After the war, Konev was made Commander-in-Chief of the Central Group Forces, in charge of administering post-war Austria. Like most prominent war heroes, Stalin moved him to a less visible position, Commander of the Carpathian Military District in 1951. After Stalin's death Konev oversaw the trial of former NKVD Chief Lavrentii Beria. Khruschev promoted him to First Deputy Minister of Defense in 1956. Later that year he was named Commander in Chief of Warsaw Pact Forces and charged with suppressing The Hungarian Revolution. Konev retired in 1960 but was quickly recalled in 1961 and made Commander of Soviet Forces in East Germany. He later served in the ceremonial role of Inspector General of the Defense Ministry. Ivan Konev died in 1971 as one of the most popular military figures in Soviet history. He is a two time Hero of the Soviet Union and is buried in The Kremlin Wall Necropolis.
